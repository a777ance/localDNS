Executive Summary

DNS — the name lookup behind every site, app, and device — is the most complete log of a household's online life, and today most homes rent that log to whoever runs their resolver.

localDNS turns it into something the household owns: a silent, ~10 W appliance — built, installed, and managed for them — that blocks ads and trackers on every device, resolves sensitive lookups privately and provably, carries the home network securely to any phone, and clears the lag that makes a fast line feel slow. The work is invisible by design, so each month every household receives a Statement that makes it visible and proves it happened. The service is delivered through the A777ance — a network of operators who each manage a book of homes.

The Problem
Your DNS log is rented, not owned
Whoever runs the resolver — ISP, public DNS, or VPN provider — sees every name the home looks up.
"Privacy" tools often just move the harvest
Consumer VPNs and Cloudflare WARP encrypt the last mile but hand your DNS to a new third party.
Home networks are noisy and slow
Ads and trackers load on every device; bufferbloat turns a fast connection sluggish under load.
DIY is powerful but unowned
Pi-hole + Unbound can do this — but few will build it, fewer will maintain it, and none of it is proven to the household.
The Solution
one node the household owns

A single low-power node runs an integrated, open-source stack — ad/tracker filtering, DNSSEC-validated recursive resolution, encrypted lookups for streaming traffic only, a self-hosted VPN, and bufferbloat control — installed, monitored, and maintained for them. Privacy you can verify: one command shows exactly which queries leave the box and which never do. Fails gracefully: if the node goes down, the router's secondary resolver keeps the home online — it loses ad-blocking, never connectivity, so the convenience never hardens into a single point of failure.

What's in the box: Pi-hole · Unbound (DNSSEC + Cloudflare-DoT split) · WireGuard · CAKE · Uptime Kuma — on refurbished hardware, ~10 W, no subscriptions in the stack.
Why We Win
Owned & verifiable
The log lives on hardware the household controls, and the privacy boundary is provable — not a promise. Hard to fake, hard to copy.
Managed, not DIY
We build, monitor, patch, and answer for it. The household gets the outcome without the maintenance.
Proven every month
The Statement is the retention engine — it renews the subscription by showing the work, not asserting it.
Graceful by design
No new single point of failure; the router's secondary DNS is the safety net under every node.
The Statement — keeping the value visible
pest control, not lawn care

The value of this service is the quiet — and quiet is easy to cancel. So the model is pest control, not lawn care: the monthly Statement is the sticker on the door that proves the work happened. Each household gets a brokerage-style page — queries resolved, threats blocked, uptime — and a "Handled For You" log of fixes made on their behalf, by name, usually before they noticed. Operators get an Alliance Member Portfolio across their whole book. Statements are honest by construction: the generator omits any figure it cannot measure rather than invent one.

Business Model & Unit Economics
two lines per home · scaling unit = the operator book

Two revenue lines per household: a one-time onboarding (hardware + install) and a recurring monthly subscription for management, monitoring, and the Statement. Marginal cost is near-zero after onboarding — the software is free, the node's ~10 W is the household's, and a richer AI-written Statement costs about a cent.

Go-to-Market — The Alliance

Distribution is the Alliance: skilled operators who each own and grow a book of homes, the way a pest-control route or an insurance book compounds. Land where the pain is sharpest — privacy-conscious households, work-from-home professionals, large connected families, and small offices. The Statement is also the marketing: its QR codes invite the household to a live status page and an opt-in directory that matches them to a profile-similar Alliance member — turning every kept document into a referral surface and a light community network effect.

Risks & The Honesty Gate
Single-node, not high-availability
Mitigated by the router's secondary DNS — the home degrades to no-ad-blocking, never to offline.
Operator-skill dependency
The build is documented as infrastructure-as-config and fully reproducible; the Alliance trains and equips operators.
Pricing unvalidated
No projection is trusted until the first paid cohort sets real ARPU and retention.
Data-source gaps
Until the volume layer is deployed, statements scope to what Pi-hole + Uptime Kuma + WireGuard already prove.
The honesty gate. Nothing aspirational ships as real. A document that goes out to a household — on paper, in an inbox, or in an app — shows only figures we can stand behind. Samples are fine; samples labeled as real are not.
What We Need Next

Bootstrapped and pre-revenue. The near-term goal is a first cohort of ~10 paying households to validate pricing and retention, the service backend to fulfill the Statement's promises, and one or two operators to prove the Alliance book travels. Capital needs are modest — refurbished hardware and time, not cloud spend.
